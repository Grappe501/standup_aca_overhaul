## **The short version (3 minutes)**

Big healthcare reforms usually fail for one of two reasons:

1.  They are politically unrealistic.

2.  They are administratively vague.

This proposal avoids both.

We do not rely on a single all-or-nothing bill.

We use existing law, phased legislation, and regulatory authority together.

The result is a reform path that:

- protects people quickly,

- shifts power over time,

- lowers costs,

- and can pass Congress without raising taxes.

## **A core reality check**

Congress does not pass perfect bills.

It passes:

- packages,

- extensions,

- pilots,

- and compromises.

Serious reform succeeds when it:

- solves urgent problems,

- fits within existing structures,

- and creates momentum instead of backlash.

This proposal is designed for that reality.

## **The legislative strategy: three tracks at once**

We pursue reform through three coordinated tracks --- not one.

### **Track 1: Immediate protection (must-pass legislation)**

This track attaches reforms to legislation Congress already has to pass.

Examples:

- Preventing premium spikes

- Extending affordability protections

- Closing high-impact loopholes

- Stabilizing rural providers

These provisions are framed as:

- cost protection,

- market stability,

- and continuity --- not ideology.

They are designed to be politically difficult to oppose.

### **Track 2: Backbone authorization (foundational legislation)**

This track establishes the new healthcare backbone.

Key features:

- Authorizes a national public or hybrid baseline plan

- Sets affordability caps

- Establishes payment frameworks

- Allows phased rollout

- Preserves private options on top

This legislation:

- defines the structure

- delegates implementation details to agencies

- avoids over-prescription that stalls passage

The goal is to set the rules of the road --- not micromanage care.

### **Track 3: Regulatory implementation (executive authority)**

Much of what people think requires Congress actually does not.

Federal agencies already control:

- Marketplace standards

- Plan certification

- Network adequacy

- Payment parameters

- Program integrity

- Enrollment processes

This project explicitly separates:

- what must be done by law

- from what can be done through regulation

That allows:

- faster implementation

- adaptation over time

- reduced legislative risk

## **Why this doesn't require new taxes**

This proposal is paid for by:

- lowering prices

- reducing waste

- eliminating rent extraction

- stabilizing risk pools

- reducing subsidy overpayment

Key cost controls include:

- closing insurer loopholes

- standardized pricing benchmarks

- reduced administrative overhead

- simplified enrollment

- better risk pooling

When prices fall, subsidy costs fall.

This is cost control --- not cost shifting.

## **The politics: who supports this and why**

This proposal is intentionally framed to build unusual coalitions.

### **Natural supporters**

- families facing high costs

- rural communities

- small businesses

- healthcare workers

- independent providers

- budget hawks concerned about waste

### **Tough-but-reachable audiences**

- moderate members from swing districts

- rural conservatives

- employer-focused lawmakers

### **Likely opponents**

- entrenched insurance interests

- middlemen benefiting from opacity

- large consolidated systems resisting transparency

Opposition is expected --- but isolated.

## **Why this avoids common reform failures**

This proposal avoids:

- overnight system replacement

- massive tax increases

- provider shock

- administrative overload

- ideological branding

Instead, it:

- builds on existing law

- phases change

- proves results

- and locks in protections

Reform becomes normal --- not radical.

## **Implementation timeline (illustrative)**

Year 1

- Affordability protections enacted

- Rural stabilization measures begin

- Regulatory groundwork laid

Years 2--3

- Backbone option launches in underserved markets

- Enrollment expands

- Payment reforms phase in

Years 4--5

- National availability

- Full affordability caps

- Mature competition

- Reduced subsidy spend

This is deliberate by design.

## **The takeaway**

This project does not ask Congress to:

- leap into the unknown

- raise taxes

- or dismantle healthcare overnight

It asks Congress to:

- protect families,

- stabilize communities,

- lower costs,

- and fix a system everyone knows is broken.

That is not radical.

That is responsible governance.
